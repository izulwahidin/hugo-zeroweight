## The Core Components: Hers Weight Loss Medications Explained

The Hers weight loss program provides access to two primary categories of prescription treatment: compounded GLP-1 injections and oral medication kits. Understanding the critical distinction between compounded and FDA-approved pharmaceuticals is essential for anyone considering this path. Typically, eligibility for these treatments is determined via an online consultation and generally requires a Body Mass Index (BMI) of **$30$ or higher**, or a BMI of **$27$ or higher** with certain weight-related health conditions such as high blood pressure or high cholesterol.

### Compounded GLP-1 Injections: What You Need to Know

The program offers access to **compounded glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) injections**. GLP-1 receptor agonists, like the active ingredients in the brand-name drugs Ozempic, Wegovy (semaglutide), and Mounjaro/Zepbound (tirzepatide), work by mimicking a gut hormone that regulates appetite and insulin release.

However, a crucial point of distinction must be understood: while these compounded injections may contain the same **active ingredient** as the brand-name drugs, the **compounded products themselves are not FDA-approved**. Compounding pharmacies create a customized medication for an individual patient based on a licensed practitioner's prescription. In response to the rise of these products, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has issued warnings. The agency states that compounded drugs do not undergo the rigorous premarket review for safety, effectiveness, and quality that is required for brand-name, FDA-approved medications. The FDA advises patients to only use compounded drugs when their medical needs cannot be met by an approved drug and has noted concerns about potential quality issues, dosing errors, and contamination with non-approved ingredients like salt forms of the active drug.

### Oral Medication Kits: Metformin, Bupropion, Topiramate & Naltrexone Combinations

For those who prefer a non-injectable option or who may not be candidates for GLP-1s, Hers offers customized oral medication kits. These kits typically use combinations of medications like **Metformin, Bupropion, Topiramate, and Naltrexone**.

Unlike the compounded injections, the medications used in these oral kits are individually FDA-approved. However, the prescriber utilizes them together in a customized combination for the 'off-label' purpose of weight management. For example, Metformin is approved for type 2 diabetes, Bupropion is an antidepressant, and Topiramate is an anti-seizure medication. When combined under the oversight of a licensed provider, they can help address various facets of weight loss, such as appetite control and metabolic function, which may be a preferred initial route for some users. This non-injection alternative offers a different mechanism of action and may be more suitable for individuals who have concerns about the side effects or logistics of injectable treatment.

## Safety First: Potential Side Effects and How to Manage Them

The Hers program, like all pharmaceutical weight loss treatments, involves managing potential side effects and adhering to strict safety protocols. A responsible approach to treatment requires understanding these risks upfront.

### Common Side Effects of Oral and Injectable Weight Loss Treatments

Whether you are prescribed a compounded glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) injection or an oral medication kit, gastrointestinal issues are the most common initial complaints. For users of GLP-1 medications, the frequent side effects include **nausea, diarrhea, and upset stomach** or vomiting. These symptoms are generally transient; they are most noticeable when a patient first begins treatment or when the dosage is increased, and they tend to subside as the body adjusts. Strategies like eating smaller, more frequent meals and avoiding high-fat or spicy foods can help mitigate these initial discomforts.

The oral medication kits, which often combine agents like Bupropion and Topiramate, carry distinct risks. For instance, Bupropion can potentially lead to **anxiety, restlessness, or insomnia**. Topiramate, an anticonvulsant utilized for its appetite-suppressing effects, is associated with more complex risks, including a potential for **kidney stones** and cognitive issues, commonly referred to as 'brain fog' or difficulty concentrating. Patients on these oral kits should be mindful of these psychological and physical side effects and communicate any persistent changes to their prescribing provider immediately. 

### Crucial Warnings: Contraindications and Severe Health Risks

Before beginning any regimen on a telehealth platform, the article must clearly advise consulting a personal primary care physician (PCP) or specialist. Given the complexity of the medications and the potential for severe adverse events—particularly with compounded GLP-1 drugs which are not FDA-approved—having a trusted, local healthcare provider aware of and monitoring your treatment is crucial for responsible care. This multidisciplinary oversight is vital to ensure patient safety and proper management of chronic conditions. The FDA has also reported that compounded GLP-1 drugs have been associated with a higher odds of adverse events, including hospitalizations, compared to the approved products, highlighting the need for extreme caution.

Crucially, **GLP-1 agonists are absolutely contraindicated in specific high-risk groups.** Individuals with a personal or family history of **Medullary Thyroid Carcinoma (MTC)** or **Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia syndrome type 2 (MEN 2)** must not use GLP-1 agonists. These medications carry a boxed warning due to the observed risk of thyroid C-cell tumors in rodent studies. While the human relevance is debated, this warning remains a standard precaution for the entire class of drugs, including compounded versions. Other severe but rare risks include acute pancreatitis and severe allergic reactions.

## Beyond Medication: The Role of Lifestyle and Ongoing Support

The impressive `before and after` results seen with Hers and other medical weight loss programs are often attributable to powerful medication, but it is a critical misconception to view the drug as a complete solution. Fundamentally, **medication is a tool** that enhances your body's ability to manage hunger and regulate metabolism. Sustained, long-term success is still fundamentally dependent on a patient's adherence to a reduced-calorie diet and increased physical activity.

### The Importance of Nutrition and Exercise in Sustained Weight Loss

Weight loss medications, particularly GLP-1 agonists, work by reducing appetite and slowing gastric emptying, making it easier to consume fewer calories. However, they do not prescribe a healthy meal plan or ensure you are getting enough movement. These lifestyle factors remain the cornerstones of success.

For those serious about long-term weight management, the focus must shift from simply losing weight to creating permanent, healthy habits. A highly effective approach to treating obesity recognizes the condition as a complex, chronic relapsing disease. As **Dr. James Ku, a board-certified bariatric surgeon and obesity medicine specialist at Penn Medicine,** emphasizes, “There is no one-size-fits-all answer to addressing a disease as complex as obesity. Successful weight loss requires a long-term commitment by both the patient and those supporting their healthy weight management journey.” This clearly underscores the necessity of a multidisciplinary approach that includes medical oversight, tailored nutrition, and consistent exercise.

### Q1. How much weight can you lose on the Hers program?

### Q2. Is the weight loss medication from Hers FDA-approved?

This is a critical distinction that requires close attention and transparency regarding the source of the medication. The oral medication kits contain ingredients like Metformin, Bupropion, and Topiramate, all of which are **FDA-approved drugs** but are being prescribed by the provider **off-label** in combination for weight management. However, the specific **compounded GLP-1 injectable medications** offered by Hers are **not FDA-approved**. Compounding pharmacies create these products, which means they have not undergone the rigorous testing by the FDA for safety, effectiveness, or quality that is mandatory for brand-name prescription drugs like Ozempic or Wegovy. The FDA has repeatedly issued warnings and enforcement guidance regarding the use of compounded versions of these drugs, especially now that the brand-name shortages have been largely resolved, underscoring the potential risk difference between approved and compounded products.

### Q3. What happens if I stop taking the Hers weight loss medication?

Weight regain is a known and highly documented outcome for many individuals who stop taking GLP-1-based medications. Clinical studies have consistently shown that once a patient discontinues GLP-1 agonist therapy, the appetite-suppressing and metabolic effects wear off, and the body’s natural biological mechanisms for weight regain, often referred to as a "weight set point" defense, resume. A major 2022 study on brand-name semaglutide found that patients regained approximately **two-thirds of the weight they had lost** within one year of stopping the medication. This medical fact emphasizes that obesity is a chronic disease requiring a long-term management strategy. To maintain weight loss after discontinuing the Hers program, patients must commit to continued, rigorous **lifestyle modifications**, including a reduced-calorie diet and consistent physical activity, as recommended by medical professionals.
