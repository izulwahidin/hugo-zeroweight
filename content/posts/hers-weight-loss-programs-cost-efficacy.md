Pharmacologically, its primary action involves slowing down **gastric emptying**, meaning food stays in the stomach for a longer period. This dual action—satiety signals to the brain and a physical delay in digestion—is the powerful combination that helps users consume less food overall. This mechanism is crucial for long-term adherence and effectiveness.

To ensure the highest standard of safety and efficacy, the medical professionals guiding this program rely on rigorous data. ## Safety and Side Effects: What to Know Before Starting Treatment

Weight management medications, particularly GLP-1 receptor agonists, are powerful tools that offer significant health benefits, but like all prescription drugs, they carry potential side effects. Understanding these risks, and how to manage them, is essential for a safe and effective treatment experience. Your Hers medical provider will discuss these in detail, but reviewing the information beforehand will prepare you for a more informed consultation.

### Common and Manageable Side Effects (Nausea, Diarrhea, Constipation)

The most frequently reported initial side effect of GLP-1 agonists is **gastrointestinal distress**, which includes nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and constipation. This is a common and expected reaction as your body adjusts to the medication, and in most cases, these symptoms will significantly diminish or resolve entirely as you remain consistent with your dosage. For instance, clinical data shows that while nausea is common early on, only a small percentage of patients discontinue treatment due to these symptoms, highlighting their generally manageable nature.

The severity of these common side effects is often linked to the initial titration period (dose ramp-up). A strategic way to mitigate this is by **adopting a bland diet for the first few weeks** of treatment. Focusing on low-fat, easy-to-digest foods—such as lean protein, toast, rice, and bananas—can reduce the workload on your slowed digestive system. Additionally, eating smaller, more frequent meals instead of large ones will help prevent uncomfortable fullness and reduce the likelihood of nausea and reflux. Staying hydrated is also crucial for managing both diarrhea and constipation.

### Serious Warnings, Contraindications, and When to Consult an an Emergency Doctor

While the common side effects are generally mild, there are critical warnings and contraindications you must be aware of for your safety. As mandated by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for this class of medication, there is a **boxed warning** about the risk of thyroid C-cell tumors. Therefore, if you or any member of your immediate family has a history of medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC) or Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia syndrome type 2 (MEN 2), you are strictly ineligible for treatment with GLP-1 agonists.

It is absolutely mandatory that you share your **full medical history** with your Hers provider during the initial screening and subsequent check-ups. This includes a detailed history of pancreatitis, kidney disease, or any current eating disorders, all of which are critical factors in determining eligibility. The Hers provider’s expertise ensures that treatment adheres to strict clinical safety guidelines.

In rare cases, serious side effects can occur, requiring immediate medical attention. You should consult an emergency doctor or seek immediate medical care if you experience:

* **Signs of Pancreatitis:** Severe and persistent pain in your abdomen that may radiate to your back, with or without vomiting.
* **Severe Allergic Reaction:** Swelling of your face, lips, tongue, or throat; problems breathing or swallowing; severe rash or itching; or a very fast heartbeat.
* **Signs of Gallbladder Disease:** Pain in the upper abdomen, fever, or jaundice (yellowing of the skin or eyes).

Prompt recognition and reporting of these symptoms are vital to protecting your health while on the program.
