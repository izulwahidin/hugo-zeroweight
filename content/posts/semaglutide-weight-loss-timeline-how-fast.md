Semaglutide works by powerfully reducing appetite and increasing the feeling of fullness. However, it does not prevent you from eating high-calorie, low-nutrient foods. ### Recognizing and Overcoming a Weight Loss Plateau

A true "plateau" is a common and normal physiological event that can occur in any weight loss journey, even one involving medication. For those on semaglutide, it is clinically defined as a period where weight loss is less than **1% of body weight per month** for several consecutive months after the patient has reached the full maintenance dose ($2.4 \text{ mg}$). This adaptation occurs because, as your body loses weight, its resting metabolic rate naturally slows down as a survival mechanism, meaning fewer calories are needed to maintain your new, lower weight. A sustained plateau warrants a physician review to rule out underlying issues and discuss strategies.

To confidently break through a plateau and maximize therapeutic benefit, patients should optimize their nutritional intake. **Accredited professionals, such as Registered Dietitians,** consistently stress the essential need to focus on two macronutrients: **protein and fiber**. A high-protein diet (often $1.2$ to $1.6$ grams per kilogram of body weight per day) is critical for **preserving lean muscle mass** while losing fat, which helps keep the metabolism from slowing down excessively. Similarly, a high-fiber intake not only aids in managing the common side effect of constipation but also maximizes the feeling of satiety, synergizing with semaglutide's mechanism of action. By prioritizing these nutrient-dense foods, you ensure that the few calories you consume are maximally effective for both fueling your body and promoting sustained weight loss.

1.  **Increased Satiety:** It signals the brain‚Äôs appetite centers, particularly in the hypothalamus, to feel fuller. By communicating to the brain that the body is nourished and satisfied, the medication reduces hunger signals and minimizes food cravings.
2.  **Delayed Gastric Emptying:** It slows down the rate at which food moves from the stomach into the small intestine. This delayed digestion helps an individual feel full for a longer period of time after a meal, making it easier to adhere to a reduced-calorie diet.
