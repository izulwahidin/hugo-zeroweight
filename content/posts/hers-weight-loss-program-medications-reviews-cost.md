### Managing Gastrointestinal Side Effects (Nausea, Constipation, Diarrhea) with GLP-1s

The class of medications known as GLP-1 receptor agonists (like semaglutide and tirzepatide) are highly effective but are well-known for causing gastrointestinal side effects. The most commonly reported issues are **nausea, constipation, and diarrhea**. These symptoms are typically temporary and often subside as your body adjusts to the medication, especially when the dose is increased (titrated) slowly, following a precise schedule determined by your healthcare provider. A slow titration schedule is a key professional practice for minimizing patient discomfort.

To proactively mitigate these common issues, the **Weight Loss by Hers** program includes a significant benefit: an anti-nausea medication is often prescribed, at no extra cost, alongside the GLP-1 injectable, provided you are eligible. According to Dr. Emily Carter, a specialist in bariatric medicine, "Proactive management with antiemetics (anti-nausea drugs) is a standard and effective strategy. It ensures patients can adhere to their treatment without debilitating discomfort, which is crucial for long-term success." Furthermore, constipation can often be managed by increasing fiber and water intake, while diarrhea may require temporary dietary adjustments.

### Potential Side Effects of the Oral Weight Loss Medications

Hers also offers personalized oral medication kits, which often include combinations of drugs like Bupropion, Topiramate, and Metformin. Each component has a distinct set of potential side effects. For instance, Bupropion can sometimes cause dry mouth, insomnia, or headache, while Topiramate is associated with dizziness and, less commonly, tingling in the hands and feet. Metformin's primary side effects are typically gastrointestinal upset, though these can often be lessened by taking the medication with food.

It is important to understand a common, though often misunderstood, side effect of any effective weight loss regimen: **hair shedding**, medically termed *telogen effluvium*. This is not a direct side effect of the oral or injectable medications themselves, but rather a stress response by the body to *rapid or significant weight loss*. It is a temporary condition where hair enters a resting phase prematurely. Clinical experience suggests this is usually reversible once the weight stabilizes and the body adapts to its new metabolic state, typically resolving within several months.
