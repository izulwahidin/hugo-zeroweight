### Clinical Criteria: Who Qualifies for Semaglutide Treatment?

The decision to prescribe semaglutide is governed by strict clinical guidelines to ensure the medication is both necessary and safe for the patient. For a patient to qualify for semaglutide treatment, they must typically meet the criteria set forth in the FDA-approved labeling for chronic weight management. Specifically, this means having a Body Mass Index (BMI) of **30 or greater** (defined as obesity), or a BMI of **27 or greater** (defined as overweight) accompanied by at least one weight-related comorbid condition, such as type 2 diabetes, dyslipidemia, or hypertension.

A qualified provider’s commitment to patient safety is demonstrated by their adherence to these guidelines, including a thorough review of potential contraindications. For example, the FDA's safety information explicitly advises against the use of semaglutide in patients with a personal or family history of **Medullary Thyroid Carcinoma (MTC)** or in patients with **Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia syndrome type 2 (MEN 2)**. A clinic with the highest standards will screen for these and other serious conditions, like a history of pancreatitis, to mitigate risk and ensure the treatment is medically appropriate.

### Titration and Dosage Adjustments: Finding the Therapeutic Dose

A key takeaway for any patient starting semaglutide is that safe and effective treatment is not an overnight process; it requires a slow, gradual dosage increase, a process known as **titration**. To minimize common gastrointestinal side effects—such as nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea—the dosage is typically increased over a period of 16 to 20 weeks until the maintenance dose is reached. Rushing this process can lead to severe discomfort, non-adherence, and ultimately, failure to achieve desired weight loss. A provider with significant experience will adhere strictly to a measured titration schedule, customizing the pace based on your body’s unique response and tolerance levels, ensuring a smoother transition to the full therapeutic dose.
