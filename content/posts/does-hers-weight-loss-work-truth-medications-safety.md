This mimicked hormone primarily targets the brain's appetite-regulating centers, particularly the hypothalamus. By acting on these regions, GLP-1 agonists effectively increase feelings of fullness and satiety, while also slowing down gastric emptying. The net result is a substantially reduced desire to consume calories, leading to significant weight loss in clinical settings.

The dramatic results seen in patients are not anecdotal; they are supported by robust, large-scale Phase 3 clinical trials. For example, the **Semaglutide Treatment Effect in People with Obesity (STEP)** trial program, a critical series of studies, demonstrated that patients receiving the therapeutic dose of semaglutide (the active ingredient in drugs like Ozempic and Wegovy) achieved an average total body weight loss that was dramatically higher than those in the placebo group. According to data published in *The New England Journal of Medicine*, participants on the active drug, combined with lifestyle intervention, consistently achieved an average weight reduction of approximately 15% to 17% over a 68-week period. This level of demonstrated clinical effectiveness is the foundation upon which the Hers program operates, ensuring that the prescribed treatment is based on the highest standards of medical and scientific evidence.

### Medical History Red Flags: Who Should NOT Use the Hers Program

Beyond the BMI and co-morbidity requirements, a thorough review of a patient's medical history is essential to rule out significant contraindications. Certain pre-existing conditions make the use of Glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) receptor agonists strictly unsafe.

Specifically, patients with a **personal or family history of Medullary Thyroid Carcinoma (MTC)** are strictly ineligible for GLP-1 treatment. The FDA mandates a boxed warning for all medications in this class concerning the potential risk of thyroid C-cell tumors, a risk seen in rodent studies. Similarly, individuals diagnosed with **Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia syndrome type 2 (MEN 2)**, a rare genetic condition that increases the risk of MTC, must not use this medication. The screening process also meticulously investigates any history of **pancreatitis** or severe **gastrointestinal disease**, as these drugs affect the digestive system and can exacerbate such conditions. A responsible clinical review, like the one offered by the medical team at Hers, includes an in-depth questionnaire and consultation to ensure these critical safety standards are met before a prescription is ever issued.

The primary mechanism of GLP-1 agonists is slowing gastric emptying—the rate at which food leaves the stomach—and signaling fullness to the brain. While this is the intended mechanism for weight loss, it can also lead to common, manageable side effects like nausea, vomiting, and constipation. Patients utilizing the Hers program must therefore become proactive managers of their digestive health.

Because GLP-1s intentionally slow the movement of food through the digestive tract, adequate hydration and fiber intake become non-negotiable for mitigating potential discomfort.
