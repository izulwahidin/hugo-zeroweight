### Building Strength: The Role of Resistance Training for Metabolism

Alongside her regular cardio, Jazz incorporated **strength training** to build and maintain lean muscle mass. This is a scientifically validated strategy for sustainable weight loss, and demonstrates a credible depth of knowledge in her approach. Muscle tissue is more metabolically active than fat tissue, meaning that increasing muscle mass directly contributes to a higher **Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR)**. In simple terms, your body burns more calories at rest the more muscle you have.

This effect is known as Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption (EPOC), or the "afterburn" effect, where the body continues to burn calories at an elevated rate for hours after the resistance workout is complete as it repairs and rebuilds the muscle fibers. By integrating strength work, Jennings didn't just burn calories *during* a workout; she optimized her body's calorie-burning machinery to work for her **24/7**, significantly supporting the long-term maintenance of her new, lower weight.

### Expert Tip: Starting a Sustainable Exercise Routine

Starting an exercise routine, especially when recovering from emotional eating patterns, must prioritize the mental relationship with movement over the number of calories burned. A certified fitness professional would advise:

> **"Focus on mindful movement, not metabolic math. Begin with gentle, enjoyable activities like brisk walking, social dancing, or simple stretching. The goal is to build a positive association with movement, viewing it as a tool for stress reduction and mood boosting—which is scientifically shown to reduce emotional eating—rather than a means to ‘earn’ food or punish yourself. Start with a non-negotiable 15 minutes, three times a week, and only increase the duration or intensity when you genuinely look forward to the next session. Consistency in joy is infinitely more powerful than sporadic intensity."**

This focus on internal motivation, self-compassion, and pleasure over punitive exercise is what allows a routine to become a life-long habit, just as it did for Jazz Jennings.
