### Optimizing Cardio: HIIT vs. LISS for Fat Burning Efficiency

The MyPhD program adopts a nuanced recommendation for cardiovascular exercise, focusing on **balancing Low-Intensity Steady State (LISS) with High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT)** on different days for optimal fat burning. Both methods offer unique metabolic advantages, and the most efficient approach is to leverage them both.

* **LISS (Low-Intensity Steady State):** This involves activities like a brisk walk, light jog, or cycling at a sustainable pace (e.g., maintaining 60-70% of your maximum heart rate) for 30–60 minutes. LISS is excellent because it places minimal stress on the central nervous system, aids recovery, and burns a higher percentage of calories directly from fat during the exercise period. The MyPhD plan often recommends LISS on recovery or non-resistance training days.
* **HIIT (High-Intensity Interval Training):** This involves short bursts of near-maximum effort followed by brief recovery periods (e.g., 30 seconds sprinting, 60 seconds walking, repeated for 15-20 minutes). While the overall calorie burn during the session might be lower than a long LISS session, HIIT creates a significant **afterburn effect** (known as EPOC, or Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption). This means your metabolism stays elevated, and you continue to burn extra calories long after your workout is finished.

By strategically alternating between LISS and HIIT, the MyPhD program ensures you tap into multiple metabolic pathways—maximizing both the immediate fat utilization of LISS and the post-exercise metabolic boost of HIIT—to compound your weekly energy expenditure and accelerate fat loss progress.
