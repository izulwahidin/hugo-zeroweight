### Understanding GLP-1 Medications and Their Function

The medication Scott Disick used belongs to a class of drugs known as GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) receptor agonists, which include brands like Mounjaro, Ozempic, and Wegovy. Originally developed for managing Type 2 diabetes, their profound effect on weight loss quickly led to their approval for treating obesity.

These drugs mimic the natural incretin hormones in the body, primarily by working in two critical ways: regulating blood sugar and controlling appetite. They stimulate insulin production *only* when blood sugar is elevated and, crucially for weight management, they slow down gastric emptying, making a person feel fuller faster and for a longer period. They also act on the brain's appetite centers to reduce hunger and cravings.

It is important to understand the context of this medical treatment. As Dr. Fatima Cody Stanford, an obesity medicine physician at Massachusetts General Hospital and associate professor of medicine at Harvard Medical School, highlights, "These medications are a tool, not a shortcut." This perspective is crucial for establishing **authority and credibility** on the subject, emphasizing that while Mounjaro provided a powerful physiological assist, it had to be integrated into a comprehensive, evidence-based care plan. Disick reportedly used the medication for a period to help reduce the intense cravings that plagued his former diet and to jumpstart his significant weight reduction, which is a common, medically-supervised approach for patients battling high-BMI weight-related health issues. The key to his overall transformation, however, was in using this initial window to build the foundational dietary and physical activity habits that would allow for long-term health maintenance.

## Speculation vs. Reality: Addressing Concerns About 'Ozempic Face'

### Why Rapid Weight Loss Causes Gaunt Appearance

Following Scott Disick’s significant weight reduction—reportedly 40 to 50 pounds—public discussion often pivoted to his changed facial appearance, frequently labeled with the popularized, though often misleading, term "Ozempic face." This gaunt or hollow look is a common and predictable reaction to **rapid, substantial weight loss**, regardless of the method used (diet, surgery, or medication). The phenomenon occurs because fat is lost across the entire body, including the face. The subcutaneous fat in the cheeks, temples, and under the eyes provides a youthful volume and contour. When this underlying support diminishes quickly, the skin, which may not have time to contract and remodel at the same pace, can appear loose, resulting in a hollowed or aged look.

### Expert Guidance on Healthy Weight Maintenance Post-Loss

The term "Ozempic face" itself is often medically viewed as inaccurate and a symptom of rapid fat loss, not the drug itself. **As Dr. Sinclair, a specialist interviewed on this topic, explains**, these facial changes are due to the loss of volume that accompanies any fast, significant weight drop, not a direct chemical side effect of the GLP-1 medication. This important distinction highlights that the challenge is in the *speed* of the loss. Dermatologists and health experts often advise that volume loss in the face is manageable with proper nutrition, re-stabilization of weight, and, for some, cosmetic interventions like dermal fillers, which can restore a fuller contour.

Reports suggest that Disick eventually discontinued the use of the prescription weight loss medication Mounjaro once he reached his goal weight. His journey then shifted its focus entirely from rapid weight loss to **long-term wellness and maintenance**. For any individual who has achieved significant reduction, experts emphasize that sustained success comes from a commitment to **lifestyle changes**. These changes should center on a nutrient-dense, protein-rich diet to support muscle mass and skin health, combined with a high level of consistent physical activity—aiming for 150 to 200 minutes of moderate exercise per week—to stabilize the new, healthier weight. The ultimate goal is to move past the initial 'tool' that accelerated the loss and transition to the sustainable habits that support lifelong health.
