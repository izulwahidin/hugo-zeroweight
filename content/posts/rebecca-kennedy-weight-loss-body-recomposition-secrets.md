### Strategic Cardio: When to Do It and How Long to Go

For anyone prioritizing strength training and muscle growth, the timing of cardio is crucial to avoid the "interference effect"‚Äîa phenomenon where certain endurance activities can potentially blunt strength and muscle adaptations. Research supports that separating your high-intensity strength and endurance work can mitigate this effect. As a coach, Rebecca Kennedy advises clients to **limit dedicated cardio to no more than 2-3 sessions per week** to ensure adequate recovery for the primary goal of muscle hypertrophy.

Practically, this means:

* **On Lifting Days:** Opt for short, low-impact cardio (like a 20-30 minute walk or a light cycle) *after* your strength session, or use a few minutes as a warm-up. This prevents pre-fatiguing the muscles needed for heavy lifts.
* **On Separate Days:** Use dedicated cardio days for longer, higher-intensity efforts. This allows the necessary molecular signaling pathways for muscle growth to operate optimally on your strength days.

### HIIT vs. LISS: Choosing the Right Intensity for Fat Burning

When you do dedicate time to cardiovascular work, understanding the difference between High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT) and Low-Intensity Steady-State (LISS) is vital for body recomposition.

* **LISS (Low-Intensity Steady-State):** This is work done at a conversational pace (Zone 2) for a longer duration. LISS is excellent for improving cardiovascular health, aiding in recovery, and burning fat without incurring the muscle damage that requires significant repair. It keeps overall systemic stress low, allowing your body‚Äôs resources to focus on muscle recovery and growth from your lifting sessions.
* **HIIT (High-Intensity Interval Training):** While HIIT is an efficient calorie-burner, it is highly taxing on the central nervous system and can compete with strength training for recovery resources. When including HIIT, it is best kept short (under 20 minutes) and placed on a non-lifting day, or as a true finisher to your session, to manage the accumulated training load.

### The Power of the 'Hike': Low-Impact, High-Effort Walking

The ultimate blend of LISS benefits with a high-effort feel is Rebecca Kennedy‚Äôs signature Treadmill **'Hike'** class format. These classes utilize high inclines (often greater than $10\%$) combined with moderate speeds. This methodology offers several unique advantages:

1.  **Low Impact:** Walking, even at a steep incline, is gentler on the joints than running, which is crucial for maximizing recovery and consistency, particularly on high-volume training programs.
2.  **High Calorie Expenditure:** Elevating the incline significantly increases the muscular demand on the hamstrings, glutes, and calves, driving up the heart rate and increasing energy expenditure far beyond flat-ground walking.
3.  **Specific Muscle Activation:** The steep grade effectively pre-fatigues or targets the posterior chain (glutes and hamstrings), which are key muscles for both lifting performance and an athletic physique.

By substituting traditional, high-mileage running with the strategic, low-impact high-incline 'Hike,' clients receive robust cardiovascular benefits and efficient calorie burn while protecting their ability to show up strong and execute **Progressive Overload** in their strength sessions. This is how cardio becomes a tool for enhanced performance and faster, sustainable results.
