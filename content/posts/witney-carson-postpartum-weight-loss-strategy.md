## Pillar 2: The Fitness Foundation—Functional Movement, Not Just Calories Burned

Carson’s structured yet flexible routine was a balanced blend of low-impact, deep-core work and meditative cardio. Her weekly schedule typically included **Pilates two to three times per week** to focus on deep core and stabilizer muscle engagement. For meditative and mood-boosting cardio, she relied on **running or long walks**. She was open about utilizing long walks, often with her son in the stroller, as a consistent way to move her body, clear her mind, and avoid the overwhelming pressure of intense, high-impact workouts too soon.

### Rebuilding the Core: Pelvic Floor Health and Postpartum Recovery Exercises

The cornerstone of Carson’s physical recovery was a deep respect for the structural changes her body underwent during pregnancy and childbirth. She understood that re-engaging the core had to start from the inside out, prioritizing the pelvic floor and the deep abdominal muscles. This focus on functional core work is validated by certified health professionals. As Suzanne Bowen, a Pilates expert and creator of BarreAmped, advises, "I do not recommend any crunches or spinal flexion exercises for postnatal fitness in the first six months or until any issues with diastasis or pelvic floor dysfunction are resolved." This expert guidance reinforces the necessity of Witney’s Pilates-heavy approach, which safely helps stabilize the pelvis and midsection.

This method of prioritizing coordination and functional movement is critical. Instead of aggressive moves, her initial recovery utilized gentle exercises like diaphragmatic breathing and pelvic tilts to restore the connection between the brain and the core muscles. By focusing on rebuilding the core system functionally, her routine ensured she wasn't just strengthening an "uncoordinated system"—an action that could have disastrous effects on long-term health and stability. Her success is a powerful endorsement of starting slow, listening to your body, and prioritizing long-term strength over short-term calorie burn.
