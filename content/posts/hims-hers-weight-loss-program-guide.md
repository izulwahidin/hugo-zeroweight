This mechanism of action is why these drugs represent a true paradigm shift in the medical management of obesity.

To demonstrate the **high professional standards** and effectiveness of this treatment path, we can look at the gold-standard clinical research. This high level of efficacy, published in reputable medical journals, underscores the foundation upon which Hims & Hers bases its programs. ## Maximizing Your Results: Lifestyle Changes to Complement Your Program

Successfully managing weight is a comprehensive endeavor where prescription medication acts as a powerful catalyst, not a standalone cure. Programs like **hims and hers weight loss** provide access to effective pharmaceutical tools, but the lasting outcomes—maintaining weight loss and improving overall health—are profoundly influenced by the patient's commitment to foundational lifestyle changes. Integrating appropriate nutrition and consistent physical activity is non-negotiable for translating medical intervention into sustained success.

### Integrating Diet and Nutrition Strategies with Medical Intervention

Medications that affect satiety and metabolism, such as GLP-1 agonists, significantly reduce appetite and food noise, making it easier to consume fewer calories. However, to achieve successful, long-term weight management, this reduced caloric intake must be paired strategically with a **calorie-appropriate, protein-rich diet**. When the body is in a significant caloric deficit, it will draw energy from both fat stores and muscle tissue. A higher protein intake is essential for signaling satiety and helping the body preserve lean muscle mass. Prioritizing foods like lean meats, fish, legumes, and dairy ensures that the weight being lost is predominantly body fat, not vital muscle.

The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) emphasizes that successful weight loss is built on a moderate, steady approach, suggesting a safe goal of losing 1 to 2 pounds per week . The CDC further recommends focusing on a dietary pattern rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean sources of protein, while limiting added sugars, saturated fats, and sodium. Adopting these habits while on medication establishes sustainable practices that will serve the patient long after the initial weight-loss phase is complete. This combination of clinical support and evidence-based nutrition ensures the treatment plan is robust, demonstrating a commitment to high professional standards and patient well-being.

### The Essential Role of Physical Activity in Sustained Weight Management

While caloric restriction dictates weight loss, physical activity is the primary determinant of both physical conditioning and long-term weight maintenance. For patients on GLP-1s, the nature of the weight lost is a critical concern. Losing weight rapidly, even with the help of medication, can lead to the loss of a significant amount of metabolically active muscle mass.

Therefore, patients on these types of medications should **prioritize strength training to preserve muscle mass** and enhance their resting metabolic rate. Muscle tissue burns more calories at rest than fat tissue, making it a critical component of preventing weight regain. This does not require hours in a gym; two to three sessions of resistance exercise per week, incorporating bodyweight exercises, resistance bands, or free weights, can make a substantial difference.

In addition to strength training, the American Heart Association (AHA) recommends at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity (or 75 minutes of vigorous activity). Incorporating both aerobic exercise (like brisk walking or cycling) and resistance training is the most effective approach to promoting overall cardiovascular health, improving body composition, and ensuring that the weight loss achieved through the **hims and hers weight loss** program is beneficial and sustainable. The combination of medically-supervised treatment with adherence to recognized health guidelines demonstrates a rigorous, authoritative approach to care.

### Q2. Is the medication prescribed by Hims/Hers the same as Ozempic or Wegovy?

This is a crucial point of distinction for patients to understand. The active ingredient prescribed by Hims & Hers—often **semaglutide**—is indeed the same as the active ingredient found in the brand-name, FDA-approved injectable pens like Ozempic (for diabetes) and Wegovy (for weight loss). The difference lies in the formulation and manufacturing process.

Hims & Hers typically dispenses **compounded** versions of GLP-1 medications. A compounded drug is a custom-prepared medication made by a licensed compounding pharmacy, generally because of a drug shortage or a medical need for a customized dose. Because compounded medications are not subject to the same rigorous, standardized FDA-approval process, including the extensive Phase 3 clinical trials that brand-name drugs undergo, there is no standardized, clinical evidence to state that the compounded product is exactly as safe, pure, or effective as the FDA-approved name brands. In fact, the FDA has issued warnings regarding the potential use of semaglutide salt forms in some compounded products, which are not the active ingredient in the approved versions. Hims & Hers works with state-licensed compounding pharmacies, and their customer data suggests meaningful weight loss is achieved, but you should discuss the specific source and formulation of your prescription with your licensed provider.
