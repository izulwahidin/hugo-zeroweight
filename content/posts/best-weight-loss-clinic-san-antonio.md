Bariatric surgery—which includes procedures like sleeve gastrectomy or gastric bypass—is generally reserved for individuals facing severe obesity. Specifically, surgical intervention is recommended for patients with a Body Mass Index (BMI) over $40$, or a BMI of $35$ or higher if they also suffer from significant weight-related health conditions (comorbidities) such as Type 2 diabetes or severe sleep apnea. ### Q2. What is the difference between a dietitian and a nutritionist at a clinic?

When you are seeking a highly reliable and qualified professional for your clinical nutrition guidance, the distinction between a Registered Dietitian (RD) and a nutritionist is paramount. A **Registered Dietitian** is the authoritative choice because they must complete a bachelor's or master's degree, finish a supervised clinical practice internship (often 1,200+ hours), and pass a national board certification exam. This rigorous process, regulated by the Accreditation Council for Education in Nutrition and Dietetics (ACEND), ensures the professional has the clinical foundation necessary to handle complex metabolic health issues. Conversely, the term **'nutritionist'** is not a regulated title in many states, and individuals using it may not have the same level of training or certification. For medical weight loss, where the goal is sustainable health improvement, only work with a clinic that employs board-certified RDs for your nutritional counseling.

### Q3. How long does a medical weight loss program typically last?

A medical weight loss program is structured in two distinct phases: the **Intensive Weight Loss Phase** and the **Maintenance Phase**, and the overall commitment is longer than many people realize. The initial intensive phase, focused on maximum weight reduction through highly structured diet, medication, and frequent physician check-ins, typically lasts between 12 and 16 weeks. However, the most critical part for sustained success is the **Maintenance Phase**. Based on clinical data showing that maintaining new behaviors requires long-term support, this phase can extend for an additional six to twelve months. This longer commitment—up to a year or more—is essential for embedding the new behavioral and nutritional changes, preventing weight regain, and solidifying the patient's long-term health transformation.
