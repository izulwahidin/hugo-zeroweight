### Understanding the Mental Health Component: What is Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BDD)?

Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BDD) is a mental health condition characterized by a distressing and impairing preoccupation with one or more perceived flaws in physical appearance that are either minor or unnoticeable to others. For Roman, who traced her struggles back to the age of 13 after a traumatic modeling agency experience, BDD caused a distorted self-perception, where she was "obsessed with trying to be thin" and constantly sought to make herself smaller, regardless of her actual size. The emotional turmoil and compulsive behaviors, such as mirror checking or excessive grooming, are hallmarks of the disorder, and Roman’s history of extreme weight-loss attempts, including having her mouth wired shut, are noted by mental health professionals as classic symptoms of the disorder’s all-consuming nature.

To provide necessary medical perspective and credibility, it is important to understand the prevalence of this condition. According to the Anxiety and Depression Association of America (ADAA), **Body Dysmorphic Disorder affects approximately 2.4% of adults in the United States**, a rate that is comparable to or even higher than common conditions like Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) and anorexia nervosa. This high prevalence, coupled with a significant association with social anxiety, severe distress, and an increased risk of suicidal ideation, underscores the seriousness of the diagnosis and provides context for the profound impact it has had on Roman’s life and health decisions.
